Daneshjou Lab Manual

Stanford University · Department of Biomedical Data Science & Dermatology

The Daneshjou Lab at Stanford is a group of interdisciplinary researchers in the Department of Biomedical Data Science and Dermatology, developing and applying AI/ML methods to solve problems in medicine and health equity. The faculty PI is Professor Roxana Daneshjou, MD, PhD.

This manual covers lab culture, policies, and practical guides for running research projects — including large-scale LLM experiments.

1. Lab Culture

Aim for progress not perfection. The enemy of done is perfect. Iterate quickly, ask questions and set frequent touchpoints!

1.1 Roxana's Guiding Principles

From Dr. Daneshjou
  • Your health and well-being are of utmost importance. I care about how you are doing. We work hard, but we do not sacrifice well-being or health.
  • Family first. Family emergencies, weddings, etc. are incredibly important. Let me know if there is something you need to take care of.
  • Honesty and integrity above all. I care about it more than "where you publish."
  • Mistakes happen. Admitting your mistakes (coding issue, results wrong, data deleted, etc.) is so important to me. I will never be mad about a mistake. Please don't hide it.
  • Quality over venue. We want to publish good work. If it's not ready for a deadline, I'd rather pick a different venue than publish something subpar.
  • Happiness matters. I want people in the lab to be happy and enjoy working together. If there is something that is hampering that, please let me know.
  • This lab is collaborative. We are here to help each other succeed. We are here to celebrate each other's successes.
  • Focus on doing good work. Good work will always shine through regardless of publication venue.

1.2 Respect for People Represented in Study Data

We hold deep respect for the communities and individuals whose data we study. Every data point represents a real person with a real story. This demands:

  • Rigorous attention to data privacy at all stages of research.
  • Thoughtfulness about how our findings may impact the communities we study.
  • Careful, respectful language when describing populations and health outcomes.
  • Awareness that data carries historical biases that we have a responsibility to understand and address.

1.3 Working Hours

We believe in working efficiently on your own schedule. There is no culture of overwork in the Daneshjou Lab. Key principles:

  • Work efficiently. Focus on productivity during your working hours rather than logging long hours.
  • Don't expect evening or weekend work. If you send messages or emails outside of normal hours, do not expect a response until the next business day.
  • Embrace time off. Take your vacations. Step away from work. Rest is essential for creativity and sustained productivity.
  • If you find yourself consistently working excessive hours, raise it with Dr. Daneshjou. This may indicate a workload issue that needs to be addressed.

1.4 Trainee Support, Access, and Accommodations

Your wellbeing matters. Stanford provides a range of support resources:

  • Mental health resources: Stanford offers counseling services through Counseling and Psychological Services (CAPS). Please use them if you need support.
  • Office of Accessible Education (OAE): If you have a disability or need accommodations, the OAE can help.
  • Accommodation letters: If you have an accommodation letter, please share it with Dr. Daneshjou so we can ensure your needs are met. This information is kept confidential.

If there is anything the lab can do to support your success, please don't hesitate to ask.

2. General Policies

2.1 Pre-Onboarding

Before officially joining the Daneshjou Lab, new members must complete the following steps:

  1. Meet with the PI. Schedule an introductory meeting with Dr. Daneshjou to discuss your interests, background, and potential projects.
  2. Get assigned a project. You will be matched with a project based on your skills and the lab's current needs.
  3. Draft a Research Plan. Using the lab's Research Plan template, write a research plan for your project. This should include aims, methods, timeline, and expected outcomes.
  4. Present in lab meeting. Present your research plan to the lab for feedback before starting work.
  5. Set up GitHub and VS Code. Create a GitHub account (if you don't have one), install VS Code, and familiarize yourself with Git basics.
  6. Set up commit signature verification. Configure GPG or SSH signing for your Git commits to ensure all contributions are verified.
  7. Demonstrate coding proficiency. Make 30+ commits to a project repository to demonstrate active engagement and basic coding workflow before being considered fully onboarded.

2.3 Post-Onboarding Requirements

Once onboarded, all lab members are expected to maintain the following ongoing commitments:

  • Slack communication. Slack is the primary communication channel. Check it regularly and respond promptly during working hours.
  • Weekly GAOIs. Submit your Goals, Accomplishments, Objectives, and Issues (GAOIs) to Roxana by Monday at noon every week. See Section 2.4 for details.
  • Weekly meetings with Project Lead. Meet with your project lead weekly to discuss progress and next steps.
  • Bi-weekly meetings with PI. Meet with Dr. Daneshjou every two weeks for individual check-ins.
  • 30+ commits per month. Maintain active coding engagement with at least 30 commits per month to your project repositories.
  • Accountability policy. If you are consistently not meeting these requirements, Dr. Daneshjou will discuss a plan with you. The lab's success depends on everyone pulling their weight.

2.4 Weekly GAOIs

Every week, submit a short GAOI update to Roxana by Monday at noon. GAOI stands for:

  • Goals: Your overarching goals for the next 6 months to 1 year. These are big-picture aspirations (e.g., "Submit paper to Nature Medicine," "Complete benchmark dataset"). They don't change often.
  • Accomplishments: What you actually did this past week. Be specific — "ran 3 experiments on GPT-4o" is better than "worked on project."
  • Objectives: Short-term goals for your current project over the next 1–2 weeks. These are concrete, actionable items.
  • Issues: Anything blocking your progress. This includes technical blockers, resource needs, scheduling conflicts, or life circumstances that are affecting your work. Don't hesitate to flag personal issues — the lab is here to support you.
Why GAOIs Matter

GAOIs keep everyone aligned. They help Roxana understand where you are, where you're headed, and where you need help — without needing a meeting for every update. They also help you stay focused and accountable to yourself.

2.5 Meetings

The lab has a structured meeting cadence:

  • Weekly with Project Lead: Discuss day-to-day progress, debug issues, plan next steps. These are your most frequent touchpoint.
  • Bi-weekly with PI: Meet with Dr. Daneshjou every two weeks for higher-level guidance, project direction, and career development.
  • Lab meetings: All lab members attend weekly lab meetings for project presentations, feedback, and knowledge sharing.
Be present during lab meetings. Default to laptops closed and phones put away. If you need to jot something down, that's fine — open your laptop, take the note, then close it again. The key is that your attention should be on the speaker, not on a screen. You shouldn't be staring at your laptop for most of the meeting. We want presenters to feel that the room is engaged and listening, not distracted. If something is unclear or you have a question, raise your hand and ask — that's always encouraged.

Come to every meeting prepared with an agenda. Share updates, questions, and blockers. Document action items and follow through.

2.6 Communication

Slack is our primary internal communication tool. Use it for quick questions, updates, sharing papers, and coordinating meetings. For longer discussions or sensitive topics, schedule a meeting or use email. Keep Slack channels organized and on-topic.

Don't let things fall through the cracks. If you message Dr. Daneshjou or a teammate and don't hear back within a reasonable time, follow up. People get busy and messages get buried — a polite bump is always welcome and expected. It's far better to nudge again than to sit idle waiting for a response that may have been missed.

2.7 Recommendation Letters

Dr. Daneshjou is happy to write recommendation letters for lab members. Please provide at least 4 weeks notice and include:

  • The opportunity you're applying for and its deadline
  • Your CV and personal statement (if applicable)
  • Specific points you'd like highlighted
  • Submission instructions

4. Data

We hold deep respect for the individuals and communities represented in our data. Every dataset contains information about real people, and we treat it accordingly.

The cardinal rule of data handling in this lab:

If you are not sure whether you can take an action with a data set, stop and ask. Always.

Additional non-negotiable rules:

  • Never download data to personal computers. Research data stays on approved, encrypted, institutional systems.
  • Never export results with fewer than 11 observations per cell. Small cell sizes can enable re-identification of individuals.

4.1 Human Subjects Training (CITI)

All lab members working with human subjects data must complete CITI training before accessing any data. This is a Stanford and federal requirement. Complete the appropriate modules for your research area through CITI Program.

Keep your certification current — it typically requires renewal every few years.

4.2 Computer Encryption and Security

All computers used for research must have:

  • Full-disk encryption enabled (FileVault on Mac, BitLocker on Windows)
  • A strong password and automatic screen lock
  • Up-to-date operating system and security patches
  • Stanford's endpoint management software installed if required

4.3 IRBs

Most research involving human data requires Institutional Review Board (IRB) approval. At Stanford, IRB protocols are managed through Stanford's IRB office.

To get added to an IRB protocol in the Daneshjou Lab, contact Sana Kidwai at kidwai@stanford.edu.

Required trainings before being added to any IRB:

  1. CITI Group 7 Training — Complete through the CITI Program website.
  2. HIPAA Protecting Patient Privacy 2025 (PRIV-2025-WEB) — Available via Axess.
  3. HIPAA Privacy for Researchers 2025 (CLIN-2025-WEB) — Available via Axess.
Tip

If the HIPAA course links don't open directly, navigate to Axess and find them under STARS (Stanford Training And Resource System).

4.4 Data at Stanford

Stanford has several institutional data resources:

  • STARR (STAnford Research Repository): Stanford Medicine's clinical data warehouse for research. Apply for access through the STARR website.
  • PHS Data Core: The Population Health Sciences data core can help with data acquisition, linkage, and management.

These are Stanford-wide resources available to all approved researchers.

4.5 Bringing Data to Stanford

If you need to bring external data to Stanford (from collaborators, public sources, etc.), you must go through the Data Risk Assessment (DRA) process. This ensures data is classified correctly and handled on appropriate infrastructure. Work with Stanford's Data Governance office to complete this process.

4.6 Data Sharing

We are committed to open science where possible. When sharing data:

  • Ensure sharing is permitted under your IRB protocol and any data use agreements.
  • De-identify data thoroughly before sharing.
  • Use established repositories (e.g., PhysioNet, Dryad, Zenodo) for public data releases.
  • Include clear documentation and data dictionaries.

4.7 Simulated Data

For development and testing, use simulated data that mimics the structure of your real data without containing any real information. This allows you to:

  • Develop and debug code without accessing protected data
  • Share code examples publicly
  • Test pipelines before running them on real data

Include simulated data generators in your project repositories.

5.5 Choosing Your Compute: GPUs vs APIs

A key strategic decision in any LLM project is whether to use open-source models on Sherlock GPUs or commercial APIs (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, etc.).

Guiding Principle

Think about what makes your results most compelling. If you can show your findings hold across both commercial and open-source models, that's a much stronger paper than results from a single model family.

Consider a dual-testing strategy:

  • Commercial APIs (GPT-4o, Claude, Gemini): Easy to use, excellent performance, but costs money and results are tied to a proprietary system.
  • Open-source models (Llama, Mistral, etc. on Sherlock): Free to run once you have GPU access, fully reproducible, and demonstrates generalizability.

The strategic question is: what makes your results most compelling? In many cases, showing that your findings replicate across model families is the strongest possible evidence.

Example

For a study with 12 configurations × 200 questions × 3 runs = 7,200 API calls, running on GPT-4o might cost $300–$500. But if you also validate on Llama-3-70B via Sherlock (free), you've doubled your evidence for zero additional cost.

6.2 Authorship

To be an author, you must substantially contribute to and agree to:

  • Conception/design, data acquisition/analysis/interpretation, drafting/critically revising the manuscript
  • Final approval of the version to be published
  • Accountability for all aspects of the work

Key lab policies on authorship:

  • Discuss authorship early. Have an explicit conversation about expected contributions and authorship order at the start of a project.
  • No new authors without PI agreement. Adding authors mid-project requires discussion with Dr. Daneshjou.
  • Authorship disputes should be raised early and resolved through open discussion.
Corresponding author. Dr. Daneshjou is the corresponding author on all lab publications. If you're new to publishing, this may feel confusing since students often handle the submission paperwork (creating accounts, uploading files, filling out forms). That's normal — you're doing the logistical work of submission, but the corresponding author is the person journals contact for decisions about the paper. If you have questions about this, talk to Dr. Daneshjou.
Always Keep Dr. Daneshjou in the Loop
  • Before submitting anywhere — discuss target journal/venue with Dr. Daneshjou and get her approval before you submit.
  • During revisions — share reviewer comments and your planned responses with her. Don't resubmit without her review.
  • At every stage — from first draft to camera-ready, Dr. Daneshjou should know where things stand. No surprises.

6.3 Preprints

Our lab policy is always post to arXiv first before or concurrent with journal/conference submission. Preprints:

  • Establish priority for your findings
  • Enable faster dissemination
  • Allow community feedback before peer review

Always get Dr. Daneshjou's approval before posting a preprint. She will review the manuscript and confirm it's ready for public release.

6.4 Types of Papers

Common paper types you may write:

  • Original research: Novel findings from your experiments or analyses
  • Review articles: Comprehensive synthesis of a research area
  • Methods papers: New tools, pipelines, or methodological advances
  • Perspective/commentary: Shorter opinion pieces on emerging topics
  • Letters/brief communications: Short, focused contributions
  • Benchmark/dataset papers: Introducing new datasets or evaluation frameworks

Avoid “salami slicing” — the practice of splitting a coherent body of work into the smallest possible publishable pieces. It dilutes impact, fragments the narrative, and wastes reviewers' time. Aim for substantial papers that tell a complete story.

6.5 Acknowledgments

Always acknowledge:

  • Funding sources (grant numbers)
  • Computing resources used (e.g., Sherlock)
  • Individuals who contributed but don't meet authorship criteria
  • Data providers and sources

7.1 Why Code Review?

Code review is a cornerstone of our research practice. It:

  • Makes better science: A second pair of eyes catches errors in logic, analysis, and implementation.
  • Catches bugs: Even experienced programmers make mistakes. Code review finds them before they affect results.
  • Validates reproducibility: If someone else can understand and run your code, it's more likely to be reproducible.
  • Makes better teams: Code review spreads knowledge across the lab, reduces bus factor, and raises everyone's coding skills.

7.2 Code Review Guidelines

All substantive code changes should go through review. This includes:

  • Data processing and cleaning scripts
  • Analysis and modeling code
  • Visualization code for publications
  • Pipeline and infrastructure code

Quick exploratory scripts and one-off analyses may skip formal review, but anything that produces a result in a paper must be reviewed.

7.3 Overview of Code Review Workflow

  1. Branch: Create a feature branch from main for your work.
  2. Develop: Write and test your code on the branch.
  3. Pull Request (PR): Open a PR on GitHub when ready for review.
  4. Review: A reviewer examines the code, leaves comments, and requests changes if needed.
  5. Iterate: Address feedback, push updates, and respond to comments.
  6. Merge: Once approved, merge the PR into main.

7.4 When to Stop for Code Review

Not every commit needs review, but certain gates should always trigger a review:

  • Data pull / cleaning: Before running analyses on cleaned data
  • Descriptive tables: Before finalizing Table 1 or summary statistics
  • Algorithms / models: Before training final models or running main analyses
  • Reporting: Before generating results that will appear in a manuscript

7.5 Pre-Review Checklist

Before requesting a review, make sure you've:

  • Used pull requests (not direct commits to main)
  • Re-run the code from a blank environment to ensure it works end-to-end
  • Followed the lab's style guide (see Section 8.2)
  • Included clear comments and documentation
  • Removed debugging code and temporary files
  • Added or updated tests if applicable

7.6 Requesting a Code Review

When you open a PR:

  • Identify the right reviewer(s): Choose someone familiar with the codebase or methodology.
  • Write a good PR description: Explain what the code does, why you wrote it this way, and what to focus on during review.
  • Link to relevant issues, papers, or documentation.
  • Highlight areas where you're uncertain and would appreciate extra scrutiny.

7.7 Giving a Code Review

When reviewing someone else's code:

  • Check correctness: Does the code do what it's supposed to? Are the algorithms implemented correctly?
  • Check readability: Can you understand the code? Are variable names clear? Is the logic easy to follow?
  • Check reproducibility: Can you run the code? Are dependencies specified?
  • Be kind: Frame feedback constructively. Use "we" language. Remember that you're reviewing the code, not the person.
  • Highlight good things: Don't just point out problems — also acknowledge clever solutions, clean code, and good documentation.

7.8 Timeline and Speed

Target a single business day turnaround for code reviews. If you can't review within a day, let the author know when you'll be able to. Prompt reviews keep projects moving and show respect for your colleagues' time.

8. Code Workflow

8.1 Team Agreements

At the start of each project, team members should agree on:

  • Programming language(s) and versions
  • Code style and formatting conventions
  • Git branching strategy
  • Code review expectations
  • Documentation standards

Write these agreements down in the project's README or a CONTRIBUTING file.

8.2 Code Style

Consistent code style makes code easier to read and review. Use automated formatters:

  • R: Follow the tidyverse style guide. Use the styler package for automatic formatting.
  • Python: Use Black for formatting and Ruff for linting. These are non-negotiable.
  • SQL: Use SQLFluff for SQL formatting and linting.

Configure these tools in your project and run them before every commit (ideally via pre-commit hooks).

8.3 Naming Conventions

Good names make code self-documenting. Use snake_case for variables, functions, and file names. Be descriptive: patient_age not x, calculate_accuracy not func1. Avoid abbreviations unless universally understood.

Folder Naming

  • Use lowercase with dashes (-) or underscores (_) for readability. Example: skin_imaging_pilot, model_outputs_2025
  • Organize broad → specific. Example: projectname_subproject_data
  • Add dates or version numbers when relevant. Example: checkpoints_v2, model_runs_2025-07
  • Avoid generic names like test, new, final, data, or your name.

File Naming

  • Be descriptive but concise. Example: discharge_evaluator_v2.py, llama_eval_2025-07-05.json
  • Use ISO date format (YYYY-MM-DD) for version tracking. Example: summary_stats_2025-07-05.csv
  • Always include a README.md file in every folder.
  • Name files to indicate content and order: 01_clean_data.py, 02_train_model.py, 03_evaluate.py

Good vs. Bad Examples

BadGoodWhy
data/skin_imaging_pilot/Descriptive, identifies the project
final_v2_FINAL/checkpoints_v2/Versioned clearly, no ambiguity
test.pytest_prompt_parsing.pySays what it tests
results.csvgpt4o_accuracy_2025-07-05.csvModel, metric, and date in the name
johns_stuff/llm_evaluation_pipeline/Content-based, not person-based

8.4 Project Organization

Organize your project with descriptive folder names:

project/
├── data/            # Data files (gitignored if sensitive)
├── src/             # Source code
├── notebooks/       # Exploratory notebooks
├── configs/         # Configuration files
├── results/         # Output results
├── figures/         # Generated figures
├── tests/           # Test files
├── .gitignore       # Always include!
└── README.md        # Project overview and setup instructions

Key rules:

  • .gitignore sensitive data. Never commit data files, credentials, or API keys to Git.
  • No absolute paths in code. Use relative paths or environment variables.
  • Be clear about which environments are protected (production, shared clusters) vs. development.

As you advance with your project, you may want to create a Product Requirements Document (PRD) to guide development. A PRD outlines the goals, scope, features, and success criteria for a project — keeping everyone aligned on what is being built and why.

8.5 Reproducible Documents with Quarto

Quarto enables you to create reproducible documents that combine code, results, and narrative. Use Quarto for:

  • Analysis reports that need to be re-run as data or methods change
  • Documentation and tutorials
  • Supplementary materials for publications

8.6 Use Git and GitHub

Every project must use Git and live on GitHub (in the lab's organization). Our workflow:

  1. Main branch is always in a working state.
  2. Feature branches for new work: feature/add-model-evaluation
  3. Pull requests for all merges to main (see Chapter 7).
  4. Meaningful commit messages: Explain why, not just what.

Additional practices:

  • Transferring repos: If you started a project in your personal GitHub, transfer it to the lab organization.
  • Releases: Tag releases for paper submissions and camera-ready versions using git tag and GitHub Releases.

8.7 Documentation

  • Comments: Explain why the code does something, not what it does (the code itself shows what).
  • README: Every repository needs a README with setup instructions, usage, and description.
  • Docstrings: Write docstrings for functions and classes, especially public APIs.

8.8 Running Code

  • Blank slate: Always test that your code runs from a fresh environment (new terminal, clean install of dependencies).
  • Pipelines: For multi-step analyses, use pipeline tools like Make, Snakemake, or Python's targets equivalent. These ensure steps run in the right order and only re-run when inputs change.

8.9 Lock Package Versions

Reproducibility requires pinned dependencies:

  • R: Use renv to create project-specific R library snapshots.
  • Python: Use uv (preferred) or pip freeze to lock exact package versions. Store these in requirements.txt or pyproject.toml.

Always include lock files in your repository.

8.10 Opt-in Practices

These practices are encouraged but not required for every project:

  • Testing: Write unit tests for critical functions, especially data processing and scoring logic. Use pytest for Python, testthat for R.
  • Docker: Containerize projects for maximum reproducibility, especially if they'll be shared or deployed. Include a Dockerfile in the repository.
  • CI/CD: Set up GitHub Actions for automated testing and linting.

10. LLM Experiment Pipeline

10.1 Overview

This guide covers everything you need to run large-scale LLM experiments — from getting API keys and cluster access, to building a robust experiment pipeline, to executing runs without wasting money or time.

Throughout this chapter, we'll use a running example: an experiment with 12 configurations × 200 questions × 3 runs = 7,200 API calls, costing approximately $300–$500 on GPT-4o. The principles here will help you execute experiments like this efficiently, cheaply, and reproducibly.

10.2 Test Locally First

Principle

Develop and debug on your laptop. Only move to a cluster when you need scale.

Your laptop is your fastest feedback loop. The edit-run-debug cycle is seconds on your machine vs. minutes on a cluster (waiting for jobs, checking logs, re-submitting). Use your laptop for all development work.

What "locally" means in practice:

  • Run your pipeline on 5 cases, not 200. The goal is to verify the pipeline works end-to-end, not to generate real results.
  • Test every component: data loading, prompt construction, API calls, response parsing, result storage, and scoring.
  • Only move to the cluster when your code runs successfully on a small sample and you need the scale of full data or GPU resources.
Example

Before running 7,200 calls on Sherlock, test with 2 configurations × 3 questions × 1 run = 6 calls on your laptop. This takes seconds and catches 90% of bugs.

Warning

Do not upload untested code to the cluster. Debugging on a cluster is slow, expensive, and frustrating. Every minute you spend testing locally saves 10 minutes on the cluster.

10.3 Modular Datasets

Principle

Design code so adding a dataset requires only a new loader and scorer.

Don't hard-code your pipeline for one specific dataset. Your experiment runner should not "know" about the internals of any particular dataset. Instead, use a modular architecture:

  • The runner orchestrates execution but doesn't know dataset details.
  • Each dataset module provides two things: a loader (returns questions in a standard format) and a scorer (evaluates responses).
  • The config file specifies which datasets to use.
Example

You start with USMLE-style questions, then want to add a pharma dataset. With modular design, you write pharma_loader.py and pharma_scorer.py, add pharma to your config, and run. No changes to the runner. Without modular design, you're rewriting if dataset == "usmle" blocks everywhere.

Warning

If you find if dataset == "usmle" in 15 places in your code, stop and refactor. This pattern makes your code fragile, hard to extend, and bug-prone.

10.4 Config vs. Code

Principle

Put all experiment parameters in config files, not hard-coded in your scripts.

Every parameter that might change between runs belongs in a config file: model names, temperature, number of runs, dataset paths, output directories, API endpoints, budget caps. Your code should read these from config, never hard-code them.

Maintain separate configs for different environments:

laptop.yaml

models: ["gpt-4o-mini"]
datasets: ["usmle_sample"]
n_cases: 5
n_runs: 1
output_dir: "./results/debug"
budget_cap: 1.00

sherlock.yaml

models: ["gpt-4o-2024-08-06", "llama-3-70b"]
datasets: ["usmle", "pharma", "derm"]
n_cases: 200
n_runs: 3
output_dir: "/scratch/users/$USER/results"
budget_cap: 500.00
Example

Run with python run_experiment.py --config laptop.yaml for testing, then switch to --config sherlock.yaml for production. Same code, different parameters. No editing source files.

10.5 Resumable Experiments

Principle

Store results in SQLite so crashes don't lose progress.

Large experiments will crash. APIs will timeout. Cluster jobs will be killed. Your pipeline must be able to resume from where it left off. The pattern:

  1. Manifest: At startup, generate the full list of (config, question, run) tuples that need to be executed.
  2. Update on completion: After each successful API call, immediately write the result to the database.
  3. Query pending on restart: When the script restarts, query the database for completed runs and only execute the remaining ones.

Why SQLite? It's a single file (no database server to manage), supports concurrent reads, handles atomic writes, and is built into Python. It's the perfect tool for experiment tracking.

Example

Your experiment crashes at 3,000/7,200 completed calls. You restart the script. It queries the SQLite database, finds 3,000 completed rows, generates the remaining 4,200 tasks, and picks up right where it left off. Zero wasted work, zero wasted money.

Warning

Do not accumulate results in memory and save to a JSON file at the end. If your script crashes at call 6,999 of 7,200, you lose all 6,999 results. Save incrementally, always.

10.6 Pin Model Versions

Principle

Use exact model version strings (e.g., gpt-4o-2024-08-06), not generic names (e.g., gpt-4o).

Generic model names like gpt-4o are aliases that point to different underlying models over time. If you run an experiment in March and re-run it in July, you may get different results because the alias now points to a newer model version.

Always:

  • Use the exact version string in your config files.
  • Store the model_id in your results database alongside every response.
  • Record the model version in your paper's methods section.
Example

A reviewer asks you to re-run your analysis. Your March experiment used gpt-4o, which pointed to gpt-4o-2024-01-25. By July, it points to gpt-4o-2024-05-13 — a different model with different behavior. Because you logged gpt-4o-2024-01-25 in your database, you can explain the discrepancy and re-run with the exact same model.

10.7 Track Costs in Real Time

Principle

Log token count and cost per call, and set a hard budget cap.

API costs can surprise you. Track them in real time:

  • What to track: Input tokens, output tokens, and cost per API call. Most APIs return token counts in the response. Multiply by the per-token price to get cost.
  • Hard cap: Set a budget cap in your config. When cumulative spending hits 90% of the cap, log a warning. At 100%, stop execution. Do not blow through your budget.
  • Store cost data in your SQLite database alongside results.
Example

Your budget is $400 for GPT-4o. After 5,000 of 7,200 calls, your dashboard shows $280 spent. You're on track. At $360 (90%), you get a warning. At $400, the script stops gracefully. No surprises on the lab credit card.

10.8 Free Models First

Principle

Validate your entire pipeline on free-tier LLMs before spending any money.

Use a testing ladder to catch bugs cheaply:

  1. Dry-run: Mock the API calls entirely. Test data flow, storage, and scoring logic without hitting any API.
  2. Free-tier models: Use free APIs (e.g., Groq's free tier for Llama models) to test the full pipeline with real API calls at zero cost.
  3. Small paid run: Run a small sample (20–50 cases) on your target model to validate results and estimate costs.
  4. Full paid run: Only now do you commit to the full experiment.
Example

You test your pipeline on Groq's free Llama endpoint. It runs 7,200 calls for free and reveals a parsing bug in your scoring function — the regex doesn't handle multi-line responses. You fix it before spending $400 on GPT-4o. That free test just saved you $400 and days of re-running.

Warning

Skipping the free-model testing stage is the most common way people waste money. "I'll just run a few on GPT-4o to test" turns into $50 of debugging. Use the testing ladder.

10.9 Scale Up in Stages

Principle

Three stages: bug-finding, signal-checking, full run.

Even after free-model testing, scale up your paid runs gradually:

  • Stage 1 — Bug-finding (5–10 cases): Run a tiny sample on your target model. Verify that API calls work, responses parse correctly, and results store properly. This should cost <$5.
  • Stage 2 — Signal-checking (20–50 cases): Run a larger sample. Check if the signal you expect is there. Are accuracy numbers in the right ballpark? Do different configurations produce meaningfully different results? If not, reconsider your experimental design before spending more.
  • Stage 3 — Full run: Only after Stages 1 and 2 confirm everything works and the signal is there, commit to the full experiment.
Example

Stage 1: 2 configs × 5 questions × 1 run = 10 calls on GPT-4o. Cost: $0.50. Everything works.
Stage 2: 4 configs × 30 questions × 2 runs = 240 calls. Cost: $15. You see a 12% accuracy difference between your best and worst configs — signal is there.
Stage 3: 12 configs × 200 questions × 3 runs = 7,200 calls. Cost: $350. Full results for the paper.

Warning

Do not jump straight to the full run. If Stage 2 shows no meaningful signal, you just saved yourself $335 and can redesign your experiment instead.

10.10 Open-Source Validation

Principle

Run your experiments on at least one open-source model to demonstrate generalizability.

Running on both commercial and open-source models provides a dual benefit:

  • Engineering validation: If your pipeline works on both OpenAI's API and a local Llama model, it's genuinely robust. Different APIs have different response formats, error modes, and behaviors.
  • Publishable data: "Our findings hold across both GPT-4o and Llama-3-70B" is a much stronger claim than "Our findings hold on GPT-4o." Reviewers love this.
Example

Your main results use GPT-4o (commercial). For validation, you run the same experiment on Llama-3-70B via Sherlock (free). Your paper now shows results across model families, strengthening every claim you make. The Llama run cost $0 and added an entire results table.

10.11 Parallelize API Keys

Principle

Use multiple free API keys sharing one database to maximize throughput.

When using free-tier APIs with rate limits, you can parallelize across multiple accounts:

  • Each API key gets its own worker process.
  • All workers share a single SQLite database as a work queue.
  • Workers atomically claim tasks from the queue (SQLite handles the locking).
  • Results are written to the same database, regardless of which worker completed them.

This architecture scales linearly: 5 keys = 5× throughput.

Example

You create 5 free Groq accounts, each with its own API key. Each key gets a worker process. Your 7,200-call experiment that would take 10 hours with one key now finishes in 2 hours. All results land in the same SQLite database, ready for analysis.

10.12 Order of Operations

Here is the recommended sequence for running an LLM experiment from start to finish:

  1. Build the pipeline locally — data loading, prompt construction, API call, response parsing, result storage, scoring. Test on 5 cases.
  2. Add config management — move all parameters to YAML config files. Create laptop.yaml and sherlock.yaml.
  3. Implement resumability — SQLite database with manifest, incremental writes, and restart logic.
  4. Add cost tracking — log tokens and costs per call, implement budget caps.
  5. Test on free models — run the full experiment on Groq or other free-tier APIs. Fix any issues.
  6. Stage 1 paid test — 5–10 cases on your target model. Verify everything works.
  7. Stage 2 signal check — 20–50 cases. Confirm the experimental signal is there.
  8. Stage 3 full run — commit to the full experiment. Monitor costs in real time.

Key insight: You should spend $0 until step 6. Steps 1–5 are entirely free (development time and free-tier APIs). Only in steps 6–8 do you spend money, and by then you've eliminated almost all risk of wasted spend.

11. Thinking About Projects

This chapter collects hard-won insights about how to think critically about AI/ML research projects—especially in the LLM and clinical AI space. These are the kinds of things that save you months of wasted work or a desk rejection if you catch them early.

11.1 Data Contamination & Benchmark Leakage

Key Question
When evaluating a model on a benchmark, always ask: "Did this model come out after the benchmark?" If so, the benchmark data could have been in the model's training set—and your "evaluation" is actually measuring memorization, not capability.

This is one of the most common blind spots in LLM research. A model that scores 90% on a benchmark it was trained on tells you nothing interesting. Watch for these signs:

  • The benchmark is publicly available on the internet (GitHub, Hugging Face, papers with appendices). If a human can Google it, a web-scraped training set probably includes it.
  • The model was trained after the benchmark was released. GPT-4's training data likely includes benchmarks published before 2023. A 2025 model likely has even more.
  • Performance is suspiciously high on well-known benchmarks but drops on novel or private test sets.
What to Do
Use held-out or private test sets whenever possible. If you must use a public benchmark, acknowledge the contamination risk explicitly in your paper. Better yet, create your own evaluation set—even a small one—that has never been on the internet. Also consider temporal splits: use cases published after the model's training cutoff date.

11.2 Baselines Matter More Than You Think

Key Question
Before building anything complex, ask: "What does the dumbest possible baseline score?" If a simple heuristic gets 80% and your fancy system gets 83%, that's not a paper—that's a rounding error.

Many AI papers in medicine skip obvious baselines:

  • Random guessing. On a 4-option MCQ benchmark, random chance is 25%. Your model needs to substantially beat that.
  • Majority-class prediction. If 70% of cases are "normal," a model that always says "normal" is 70% accurate. Your model's 75% accuracy is not impressive.
  • Simple keyword matching or regex. Before using an LLM to classify text, try a regex or keyword list. You'd be surprised how often it works.
  • Retrieval without generation. Before building RAG, try just returning the top retrieval result. If retrieval alone solves 90% of cases, the generation step may not be adding value.
Common Mistake
Comparing your system only against other complex systems. Reviewers will ask "did you try just prompting the model directly without your framework?" If you didn't, your paper has a hole.

11.3 The "So What?" Test

Key Question
After describing your result to someone, if their first reaction is "So what?"—you have a framing problem, a significance problem, or both.

Every project should be able to answer in one sentence: who cares about this result, and what would they do differently because of it?

  • "LLMs can answer medical questions" — known since 2023. So what?
  • "LLMs answer medical questions differently depending on the stated race of the patient, even when race is clinically irrelevant" — that changes how we deploy these systems. That matters.
  • "Our model gets 2% higher accuracy" — probably noise. So what?
  • "Our model gets comparable accuracy but runs 100x cheaper, enabling deployment in low-resource clinics" — now you have a story.

Run this test on your project before you invest months of work, not after. If you can't articulate why anyone should care, redesign the experiment.

11.4 Negative Results Are Results

Key Question
If your experiment shows that something doesn't work, ask: "Is this surprising? Would the community have expected it to work?" If yes, your negative result is publishable and valuable.

Don't throw away a project just because the hypothesis was wrong. Some of the most impactful papers show that a widely believed approach doesn't actually work. But a negative result needs:

  • A clear a priori hypothesis that was reasonable (not a straw man).
  • Sufficient statistical power to rule out the effect (not just "we tried 10 cases and it didn't work").
  • Exploration of why it didn't work. The explanation is often more interesting than the original hypothesis.
Example
You hypothesize that chain-of-thought prompting improves diagnostic accuracy. It doesn't. But digging deeper, you find it does improve accuracy on complex multi-step cases while hurting accuracy on simple pattern-recognition cases (by overthinking). That nuance is a much better paper than "CoT helps."

11.5 Confounds in LLM Evaluation

Key Question
When you see a difference between conditions, ask: "Is there any other explanation besides the one I'm testing?" LLM experiments have unique confounds that traditional ML doesn't.

Watch out for:

  • Prompt length confounds. If Condition A has longer prompts than Condition B, differences in performance could be due to context window effects, not your intervention. Control for prompt length or at least report it.
  • Order effects. LLMs are sensitive to the order of items in a prompt. If you always list options in the same order, the model may have a positional bias. Randomize option order across runs.
  • Temperature and sampling. At temperature=0, you get deterministic outputs but miss the variance. At temperature>0, you need multiple runs per case to estimate the distribution. One run per case at temperature=1 is noise, not data.
  • API version drift. If you ran half your experiment in January and half in March, the model behind the API may have changed (see 11.6 Pin Model Versions).
  • Prompt sensitivity. Small wording changes can swing accuracy by 10%+. If you only tried one prompt phrasing, you're measuring your prompt-writing skill, not the model's capability. Try at least 2–3 prompt variants.

11.6 Clinical Framing: Think Like a Doctor, Not an Engineer

Key Question
Ask: "Would a clinician actually use this? In what workflow? What would they do with the output?" If you can't answer concretely, your project may be technically interesting but clinically irrelevant.
  • Accuracy isn't everything. A model that's 95% accurate but takes 30 seconds per query won't be used if the doctor can answer in 5 seconds. Think about when and where in the clinical workflow AI adds value.
  • Failure modes matter more than average performance. A model that's 98% accurate but hallucitates drug interactions 2% of the time is dangerous. Characterize the failure modes, not just the mean accuracy.
  • Ground truth is messy in medicine. Two expert dermatologists disagree on diagnoses ~20-30% of the time. If your "ground truth" is one doctor's opinion, your model is being evaluated against noise. Report inter-annotator agreement.
  • Demographic subgroup analysis is not optional. If your model works great on light skin but poorly on dark skin, that's not a footnote—that's the main finding. Always break results down by available demographic variables.
Common Mistake
Building a "clinical AI tool" without ever talking to a clinician about whether they'd use it. Talk to potential users early—ideally before you write a line of code.

11.7 Anticipate Reviewer Objections

Key Question
Before you submit, play devil's advocate: "If I were a skeptical reviewer, what would I attack?" Then run the experiment that addresses that attack before you submit.

The most common reviewer objections in LLM/clinical AI papers:

  • "You only tested on one model." → Test on at least two model families (see 11.10 Open-Source Validation).
  • "Your test set is too small." → Power analysis or confidence intervals. Show that your sample size is sufficient to detect a meaningful effect.
  • "You didn't compare against [obvious baseline]." → Include simple baselines (see 12.2).
  • "How do you know it's not data contamination?" → Address this head-on (see 12.1).
  • "This is just prompt engineering." → If your contribution is a prompting strategy, you need to show it's robust across prompt variants, not just one magic prompt.
  • "What about fairness/equity?" → Report subgroup performance. If you don't have demographic data, say so and flag it as a limitation.

It's much cheaper to run an extra experiment now than to get a revise-and-resubmit (or rejection) and have to do it 4 months later.

11.8 Scope Creep: Know When to Stop

Key Question
Ask yourself: "What is the minimum set of experiments that tells a complete story?" Do those experiments. Write the paper. Then stop.

Research projects expand to fill available time. A common failure mode:

  1. You get your main result — it's good.
  2. You think "but what if I also tried X?" — you run X.
  3. X raises question Y — you run Y.
  4. Three months later, you have 47 experiments and no paper.

The right time to stop experimenting and start writing is when you can tell a coherent story with the results you have. Additional experiments are always possible. The question is whether they change the story or just add footnotes.

A Good Rule of Thumb
If you can fill a results table with 3–5 experiments that support a clear thesis, you have a paper. Everything else is either (a) a follow-up paper or (b) a supplementary experiment you run only if a reviewer asks.

12.4 Slide Design Principles

Key Principle

Slides should have the minimum amount of text possible. If you can say it out loud, don't write it on the slide. Slides are a visual aid, not a teleprompter.

  • One idea per slide. If a slide needs a paragraph of text, it should be two slides.
  • Use visuals over text. A well-designed figure, diagram, or table communicates more than bullet points.
  • Large fonts. If the person in the back row can't read it, the font is too small. Body text should be at least 24pt.
  • Consistent styling. Use the same fonts, colors, and layout patterns throughout your deck.
  • White space is your friend. A clean slide with breathing room is always better than a crammed one.
  • No walls of text. If you find yourself shrinking text to fit, you have too much content on the slide. Split it.

12.5 Delivery & Pacing

Key Principle

Always walk your audience through content one piece at a time. Never put up a slide with 3 figures and try to explain the broad concept. Walk through each graph, each metric, each result individually.

  • One figure at a time. If you have multiple panels, reveal them sequentially and explain each one before moving to the next.
  • Narrate the axes first. When showing a graph, always start by explaining what the x-axis and y-axis represent before discussing the data.
  • Pause after key points. Give the audience time to absorb important findings before moving on.
  • Tell a story. Structure your talk as: motivation → question → approach → results → implications. The audience should always know why they're looking at what they're looking at.
  • Practice out loud. Reading slides silently is not practice. You need to hear yourself say the words and time yourself.

12.6 Common Presentation Mistakes

Avoid These
  • The data dump slide. Putting up a table with 50 numbers and saying "as you can see..." — no one can see. Highlight the 2–3 numbers that matter.

  • Rushing the last third. Time your talk so you don't have to speed through results and conclusions — those are the most important parts.

12.8 Publication-Ready Data Visualization

Figures are often the most important part of a paper. Ensure your visualizations:

  • Are clear and self-explanatory with complete labels and legends
  • Use colorblind-friendly palettes
  • Meet the journal's resolution and format requirements
  • Use vector formats (PDF, SVG) when possible for crisp rendering
  • Have consistent styling across all figures in a paper

13. FAQs

How do I get added to the Daneshjou Lab GitHub organization?

Reach out to bikia@stanford.edu. See the Onboarding Checklist for the full setup process including GitHub requirements.

How am I added to lab meeting calendar invites?

Contact daneshjoulab@stanford.edu. This is part of the Onboarding Checklist (step 10) and is typically done once all prior onboarding tasks are complete.

How am I added to the Daneshjou Lab Google shared drive?

Contact daneshjoulab@stanford.edu. This is part of the Onboarding Checklist (step 11).

How do I request API keys (e.g., OpenAI, Claude, etc.)?

Email daneshjoulab@stanford.edu with subject line "SECURE: [DaneshjouLab] API Key Request". Include a brief description of your task and the model you intend to use. Keys are sent securely via Stanford Secure Mail.

How do I get access to Google Cloud Platform (GCP) for a specific project?

After being granted permission to access the project on GCP and added to the respective IRB, reach out to daneshjoulab@stanford.edu.

How do I get IRB approval to access PHI data?

See Chapter 4 (Data) for full details on IRB and data access. In short: contact Sana Kidwai at kidwai@stanford.edu. You will need to complete:

  1. CITI Group 7 Training (citiprogram.org)
  2. HIPAA Protecting Patient Privacy 2025 (PRIV-2025-WEB) — via Axess
  3. HIPAA Privacy for Researchers 2025 (CLIN-2025-WEB) — via Axess

If the HIPAA course links don't open directly, find them in Axess under STARS.

Who is the corresponding author on lab publications?

Dr. Daneshjou is the corresponding author on all lab publications. See Section 6.2 (Authorship) for details on authorship policies and the submission process.

Where should I post preprints?

Always post to arXiv first, but get Dr. Daneshjou's approval before posting. See Section 6.3 (Preprints).

How do I access Sherlock or OAK?

See Section 5.1 (Sherlock) and Section 5.2 (OAK) for access instructions, documentation links, and best practices.
